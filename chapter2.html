This chapter contextualises the development of a stroke rehabilitation device through a comprehensive overview of existing approaches to lower limb rehabilitation in stroke patients, including both traditional and device-assisted rehabilitation.

2.1 Challenges in Stroke Rehabilitation

Lower limb rehabilitation places substantial physical and time demands on physiotherapists. This burden contributes to high levels of burnout, with physiotherapists reporting stress from long working hours and the physically intensive nature of hands-on therapy (Nair, 2023).

2.2 Stroke Rehabilitation Methods

Stroke rehabilitation typically integrates manual therapies and robotic-assisted systems across different stages of recovery.

Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation (PNF)

PNF is a widely used manual technique that supports neuromuscular re-education through therapist-guided, multi-planar movements that mimic real functional tasks, making it particularly effective in the acute and subacute phases for improving balance, coordination, and mobility (Li, 2022; Nguyen, 2022).

Robotic Rehabilitation Systems

Robotic rehabilitation systems — such as exoskeletons, Body Weight Supported Treadmill Training (BWSTT) with Device Guided Orthoses (DGO), and end-effector platforms — are typically introduced in the late subacute to chronic stages. These systems deliver repetitive, task-specific movement training aimed at improving gait, walking performance, and overall functional independence.

Continuous Passive Motion (CPM)

CPM machines are also implemented early in recovery to provide repetitive movement that promotes neuroplasticity and enhances post-stroke outcomes (Ripin, 2018).

2.3 Shortcomings of Current Rehabilitation Methods

Despite their benefits, manual, CPM, and robotic approaches all face notable limitations.

Method Key Limitation
Manual (PNF) Depends heavily on therapist skill and consistency; variable treatment quality; limited standardised outcome tracking and long-term progress monitoring.
CPM Devices Support neuroplasticity but lack active resistance motion (ARM), essential for muscle engagement and improving range of motion (Indrawati & Sajidin, 2018).
BWSTT / DGO Still require substantial physiotherapist involvement during early rehabilitation, limiting scalability.
End-Effector Robots Less suitable for early-stage patients confined to supine or semi-Fowler positions, as they require partial body-weight support.

Key Gap Across all modalities, there remains a need for accessible, standardised, data-integrated solutions that support acute to late-subacute recovery, reduce therapist workload, and enable meaningful progress tracking.

2.4 Interviews with Stroke Rehabilitation Specialists

The team engaged a physiotherapist from the Stroke Support Station (S3) and an occupational therapist from Woodlands Health to understand rehabilitation workflows across acute to late sub-acute settings. An on-site visit to S3 further informed understanding of the rehabilitation environment.
