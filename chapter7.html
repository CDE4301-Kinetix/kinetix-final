Project Summary

This project presents the development of Kinetix, a robotic lower-limb rehabilitation device designed to support stroke patients through controlled, data-driven rehabilitation exercises. The device integrates mechanical actuation, multi-modal sensing, embedded electronics, and an interactive user interface into a single portable platform.

The project addressed a clear clinical gap: stroke rehabilitation in Singapore and globally depends heavily on manual physiotherapy that is physically demanding, difficult to scale, and limited in its ability to quantitatively track patient recovery. Kinetix was developed to reduce therapist workload, improve consistency of therapy delivery, and enable objective, real-time monitoring of patient progress.

Impact and Visibility

  • Mechanical system: Foldable frame design supporting 6 exercise types across supine and semi-Fowler positions; arc rail mechanism for lateral movement; ball-screw linear actuator for hip/knee/ankle exercises. FEA confirmed FoS of 28 for the aluminium tube structure.
  • Sensing system: FIMU concept selected combining 4 IMUs (lower back, thigh, shank, foot) with 17 FlexiForce A301 sensors for plantar, medial-lateral, and thigh force capture. Calibration curves established for voltage-to-force conversion.
  • Electrical system: Raspberry Pi Pico W2 MCU selected for built-in Wi-Fi/Bluetooth and strong price-to-performance ratio. Nema 23 Bipolar Stepper Motor confirmed with 3.0 Nm holding torque. LDO verified to output 3.3 V with <0.1% deviation.
  • User interface: Two iterations of UI development; final design features a therapist-focused 5-stage workflow with gamification, tablet rotation for client-facing session view, and AI-generated progress reports.

Current Limitations

Known Limitations
  • FEA analysis covered only a single loading configuration and pivot point — multi-point analysis still required.
  • Lateral stability test showed ~0.4 mm displacement and structural slanting under 9.6 kg load — addressed by design iteration but full re-testing needed.
  • Force sensor calibration was limited to small weights due to small sensing area of A301 sensors.
  • Motor functionality testing was conducted without a load and for a short duration only.
  • MCU GPIO pin count may be insufficient for all force sensors and motors simultaneously — requires schematic redesign or MCU upgrade.
  • UI testing was conducted with a small sample of physiotherapists, not in a full integrated session flow.
  • Clinical trials and formal medical device certification are outside the project scope.

Future Work

Recommended future development directions include:

  • Extended FEA: Conduct simulations at multiple pivot locations and loading conditions to fully characterise structural performance.
  • Lateral stability redesign: Validate the revised arc rail repositioning under full load range with repeated cycling tests.
  • Full sensing integration: Deploy the complete plantar force sensor array on the footplate; conduct full-foot load simulation and stepping motion tests.
  • MCU pin expansion: Redesign the electrical schematic to accommodate all 17 force sensors plus motor control, or select a higher-pin-count MCU.
  • Long-duration motor testing: Stress-test the Nema 23 motor under realistic load conditions for extended duration to confirm reliability.
  • Full system integration test: Conduct active and passive mode performance tests with both artificial and real lower limbs; validate safety override functionality.
  • Clinical validation: Pursue ethical approval for physiotherapist–patient mock sessions incorporating real sensor data; expand UI testing sample size.
  • Regulatory pathway: Explore IEC 60601 compliance requirements and develop a roadmap toward medical device certification for clinical deployment.
  • AI reporting: Integrate the post-session AI-generated report feature using biomechanical data captured during rehabilitation sessions.
