A1 · Aluminium Tube Load Test (FEA)

Figure 42
SolidWorks FEA result — aluminium tube under distributed load. Factor of Safety distribution shown: Min FOS = 28 (green/blue region). Load arrows applied along top face; fixed supports at tube ends. Colour scale from 2.808e+01 (min, red-end) to 1.087e+04 (max, blue).

Figure 42. FEA Factor of Safety distribution for aluminium support tube.

Test Procedure

  1. Identify the structural component — aluminium support tube selected as reference.
  2. Model structural geometry in SolidWorks FEA environment.
  3. Apply boundary conditions representing fixed supports.
  4. Determine loading: maximum assumed leg weight = 20 kg.
  5. Convert load to force: 100 N applied as representative loading condition.
  6. Divide load equally across two parallel support tubes: ~10 kg per tube.
  7. Run simulation; determine stress distribution and Factor of Safety.
Result Minimum Factor of Safety (FoS) = 28. The tube would require approximately 28× the applied load before structural deformation occurs. This greatly exceeds the minimum FoS of 2 specified in design requirements.

A2 · Lateral Stability Test

Test Procedure

  1. Assemble arc rail mechanism with slider and supporting structure.
  2. Apply vertical load of 9.6 kg to simulate lower limb load during operation.
  3. Measure displacement of arc rail structure under applied load.
  4. Observe any structural deformation or instability.
  5. Record displacement and inspect alignment of connected structural components.
Result & Observation Displacement recorded: ≈0.4 mm, indicating potential long-term deformation under repeated loading. The structure connected to the arc rail slider was observed to be slanted, likely due to insufficient structural support or small contact surface area between components, which may compromise long-term stability.
Figure 43
Three photographs from lateral stability test: (left) arc rail assembly with ruler showing unloaded displacement; (centre) loaded position measurement; (right) actual lower limb on device showing strapping and structural alignment assessment.

Figure 43. Lateral stability test photographs — displacement measured with ruler under 9.6 kg load.

A2 · Design Iteration — Arc Rail Repositioning

To address the slanting issue, the design was modified by relocating the arc rail beneath the linear actuator and the main structural sub-assembly. This configuration:

  • Improves load distribution by allowing the structural frame to directly support applied forces.
  • Reduces bending moments acting on the arc rail slider.
  • Increases the effective contact area between structural components.
  • Improves overall stability and reduces the likelihood of deformation during operation.

Test Shortcomings Each A301 sensor has a small sensing area, restricting calibration range. Future testing will use the full plantar sensor array on the foot plate for wider load ranges, and include a simulated stepping motion to better reflect real usage conditions.

B2 · IMU Orientation Response Test

The IMU module was mounted on a smartphone and rotated through six orthogonal orientations (±X, ±Y, ±Z axes). Sensor outputs were recorded and compared against expected gravitational acceleration values.

The test verified orientation accuracy, sensor responsiveness, and potential drift in angular measurements.

B3 · IMU Joint Accuracy Test

IMU modules were attached to relevant limb segments. Reference joint angles were measured using a protractor and simultaneously recorded with pose-estimation tracking software. IMU-derived angles were compared to both reference methods to calculate angle error across the intended ROM.

Test 2 · Nema 23 Motor Functionality Test

The DM556T motor driver was configured in a common anode setup:

  • PUL+ and DIR+ connected to 3.3 V pin of MCU.
  • PUL− and DIR− connected to MCU GPIO 0 and GPIO 1 pins respectively.
  • Motor voltage supply: 24 V DC power supply.
Figure 52
Motor functionality test setup — DC power supply (showing 24.093V / 16.67A output), DM556T motor driver (black box with terminal connections), Nema 23 stepper motor, MCU (Raspberry Pi Pico W2), laptop for serial monitoring.

Figure 52. Nema 23 Stepper Motor and DM556T Motor Driver functionality test setup.

Result Varying the steps per revolution affects motor speed — a lower steps per revolution yields faster motor speed. This allows adjustment of horizontal and lateral speed for rehabilitation exercises. The Nema 23 successfully operated under the 24V supply, confirming compatibility with the DM556T driver.

UI Testing Shortcomings Feedback was gathered using early prototypes and relied on a small number of physiotherapists, reducing diversity of perspectives. Client-side interactions (gamified elements, visual cue clarity) were tested in isolation rather than within a full session flow. Future testing should involve a larger pool of physiotherapists and clients together, incorporate real sensor data, and include full therapist–client mock sessions.
