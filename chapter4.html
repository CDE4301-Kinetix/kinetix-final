To address this issue, the design was modified by relocating the arc rail beneath the linear actuator and the main structural sub-assembly. 4.1.8 Mechanical Design Short comings

4.1.8.1 Middle Structure Section

While assembling the overall device for the first time there were issues with the stability middle section with the supports starting to bend due to the vertical load. On top of that, the mounting holes for the section are hard to reach thus making it hard for any maintenance (Figure 29).

Figure 28.

To resolve this issue, ribs were added to the structure and mountings were shifted towards a more accessible angle.

4.1.8.2 Belt Lateral mechanism

During the development phase, tightening issues were encountered with the belt used for the lateral mechanism (Figure 30).

Figure 29.

The mechanism consists of two pulleys connected by a timing belt, with one pulley mounted to the stepper motor and the other to the main structure. To address tensioning issues, a belt tensioner was initially designed and implemented using 3D-printed components (Figure 31).

Figure 30.

The 3D printed parts did not manage to sustain the friction and bending force. Thus, we opted to use a shorter timing belt which resolved this issue.

4.1.8.3 Lateral Driving Shaft

Another issue encountered involved the lateral shaft connected to the main structure for side-to-side motion. Initially, a shaft torque lock was used to secure the shaft through outward expansion; however, this was later replaced with a nut-based fastening method to provide a more stable and reliable fixation (Figure 32).

Figure 31.

The limitation of this design was that it relied solely on friction between the 3D-printed components and the shaft, resulting in insufficient clamping force and slippage. To resolve this, the system was revised to use a flange coupler with mounting holes, and the shaft was changed to a 6 mm round shaft with a field D-profile end to improve torque transmission and mechanical locking (Figure 33).

Figure 32.

The D-profile was introduced to provide a flat surface for the flange coupler’s set screw, ensuring a more secure grip and preventing slippage. As a result, the mechanism was able to achieve lateral movement.

4.2.4 Test Shortcoming and Proposals

The observed angular errors are suspected to arise from reliance on accelerometer data alone, which is sensitive to dynamic acceleration components during movement. When the limb is in motion, non-gravitational accelerations are superimposed on the gravitational signal, causing the computed joint angles to deviate from the true orientation.

To address this, future iterations should incorporate gyroscope data alongside the accelerometer through a sensor fusion approach such as a Kalman filter, which combines the low-frequency accuracy of the accelerometer with the short-term precision of the gyroscope to produce more stable and reliable angle estimates across the full range of motion.

Additionally, the current test configuration did not constrain the physical mounting of the IMU modules, introducing variability in sensor tilt and alignment between trials. A dedicated IMU holder, designed to mount rigidly onto the device frame and align each sensor consistently with the limb segment axis, would reduce placement-dependent error and improve repeatability across patients and sessions. The design of such a holder is proposed as part of the sensing subsystem iteration.

4.2.7 Footrest Design

Following confirmation of the suitability of the load cells, the footrest subsystem was designed to fulfill both mechanical and sensing requirements within the lower-limb rehabilitation device. The key functional objectives were to:

  • Withstand plantar and lateral forces applied during rehabilitation exercises
  • Enable accurate measurement of plantar and lateral forces
  • Accommodate a wide range of user foot sizes based on anthropometric data
  • Minimise structural deformation to preserve sensing accuracy
  • Integrate seamlessly with the selected force-sensing system

These requirements formed the basis for evaluating each design iteration (Figure 41).

Figure 41.

4.2.7.1 Iteration 1: Initial Prototype

The initial design, developed during the interim phase, featured a compact footrest. While the design was lightweight and simple to implement, it was observed that the footrest area was insufficient to accommodate users with larger foot sizes (Figure 42).

Figure 42.

As shown, this resulted in improper foot positioning, which affected both usability and measurement consistency. Consequently, the design did not meet anthropometric requirements and required further modification.

4.2.7.2 Iteration 2: Anthropometric Scaling

To address the limitation in foot coverage, the footrest length was increased to 32 cm based on anthropometric data of Singaporean populations through research done by Lee et al. (2015). This adjustment ensured that the design could accommodate the majority of users.

However, experimental testing revealed that the increased length led to structural deformation under load. The footrest exhibited noticeable warping when subjected to plantar forces, which adversely affected the reliability of the force-sensing subsystem (Figure 43).

Figure 43.

This iteration demonstrated that while anthropometric requirements were satisfied, mechanical stability was compromised.

4.2.7.3 Iteration 3: Structural Optimisation and Reinforcement

To resolve the structural instability, the footrest length was reduced from 32 cm to 26.2 cm. In addition, an aluminum plate and two aluminum ribs were introduced to reinforce the structure (Figure 44).

Figure 44.

These modifications were guided by mechanical principles, where reducing the span length decreases bending deformation, and the addition of reinforcement increases structural stiffness. The revised design achieved a balance between user accommodation and mechanical integrity.

Testing showed a significant reduction in deformation, resulting in improved stability and more reliable force measurements. Although there was a slight reduction in maximum foot size accommodation, the design remained suitable for the intended user population.

4.2.7.4 Iteration 4: Sensing System Redesign

During development, an alternative sensing approach was evaluated. The initial use of thin-film force sensors was found to be limited by high percentage error rates. As a result, the sensing system was redesigned to incorporate load cells.

The new design was inspired by the operating principle of electronic weighing scales, which utilise ground reaction forces for measurement. As shown in the diagram, the load cells were positioned such that applied forces from the foot are transmitted through the structure to generate measurable signals. In addition, groves were also made on the structure for the circular section of the load cells to slip in to prevent slipping (Figure 45).

Figure 45.

This transition significantly improved measurement accuracy, repeatability, and overall system robustness.

4.2.7.5 Iteration 5: Integration and System Refinement

Following the adoption of load cells, further refinements were made to optimise system integration. A dedicated compartment was incorporated within the footrest to house amplifiers, reducing wire length and minimising the risk of wire damage. Additionally, the position of the Velcro strap slots was shifted from the centre to the upper region of the footplate (Figure 46).

Figure 46. Full integrated images of the footplate (left), close-up view of load cells in grooves (right)

The final footrest design integrates both structural and sensing considerations. The reinforced structure ensures minimal deformation under load, while the load cell-based sensing system provides accurate and reliable force measurements. The design also incorporates improved user interface features, such as optimised strap placement and adequate foot support, enhancing overall usability.

The CAD model below illustrates the integration of the ankle-sensing component. The design comprises two plates mounted onto a U-shaped bracket with a controlled clearance. This allowance enables the plates to translate towards the bracket under applied load, thereby generating a measurable normal force. The same force transmission principle is applied in the plantar force sensing mechanism to ensure consistent and accurate force detection (Figure 47).

Figure 47. CAD Assembly of foot plate structure

Each iteration addressed specific limitations identified through testing, resulting in continuous improvement.

Key advancements include the following:

  • Improved anthropometric compatibility
  • Enhanced structural stability through reinforcement
  • Transition to a more reliable sensing system
  • Optimised integration of mechanical and electrical components

Overall, the final design reflects a well-evaluated and comprehensive approach, integrating engineering principles, experimental validation, and user requirements.

4.2.8 Thigh force sensing Design

The thigh subsystem was designed to fulfill sensing requirements within the lower-limb rehabilitation device.

The key functional objectives were to:

  • Enable accurate measurement of flexion forces
  • Accommodate a wide range of user thigh sizes based on anthropometric data

To ensure effective force transmission and user compatibility, the contact interface was designed with reference to thigh circumference anthropometric data adapted from Nike.com. Based on this data, a contact arc of 60 degrees was derived to provide sufficient surface contact with the thigh during force exertion. This design ensures measurement accuracy while maintaining user comfort across different body sizes (Figure 48).

In addition, similarly to the footrest design, the thigh force sensing structure also has an electrical housing for the amplifiers that connects to the two load cells.

4.2.9 IMU Holder Design

The IMU holder was created to serve two purposes (Figure 49):

  • Remain stable during the movement of the structure to reduce data-recording errors
  • To serve as a protection against mechanical damage and splatter from wipe-down.

Figure 49. A side-by-side comparison of IMU iteration. Old design (left), Final design (right)

Iteration 1: Initial Holder Design

The initial IMU holder was designed with a flat base and enclosed structure to house and protect the sensor. This configuration provided adequate physical protection and ensured that the IMU was securely contained within the holder.

However, during testing under movement conditions, it was observed that the flat base did not conform well to the curved surface of the aluminum tube. This resulted in reduced contact area and insufficient frictional resistance, causing slight instability and potential displacement of the IMU during operation.

As shown in Figure 49, this limitation highlighted the need for improved geometric compatibility between the holder and the mounting surface.

Iteration 2: Curvature-Based Design

To address the instability observed in the initial design, the base of the IMU holder was redesigned to incorporate a curvature that matches the arc of the aluminum tube.

This modification increased the contact surface area between the holder and the mounting structure, thereby improving frictional grip and mechanical stability. The improved conformity reduced micro-movements of the IMU during dynamic operation, leading to more reliable and consistent data acquisition.

Additionally, the enclosed structure was retained to continue providing protection against mechanical impact and environmental exposure.

Figure 50. Image of IMU with holders attached on structure

4.2.10 Ultrasonic Sensor Holder Design Iteration

The ultrasonic sensor holder was created to serve two purposes (Figure 51):

  • To place the sensors at strategic locations to detect the structure
  • To serve as a protection against mechanical damage and splatter from wipe-down.

Figure 51. Design of ultrasonic sensor holder for lateral movement

In order for the ultrasonic sensor to be able to detect the lateral movement of the structure to prevent it from going out of range, it needs to be elevated to the same level as the aluminum rail. This elevation ensured that the emitted ultrasonic waves are directed accurately towards the rail, allowing reliable detection of lateral displacement and preventing the structure from moving beyond its intended range (Figure 52).

Figure 52. Design of ultrasonic sensor holder for linear movement

The design for the ultrasonic sensor to detect the linear motion involves designing the holder around the NEMA motor structure to ensure good fit for stability.

In active mode, the device responded appropriately to user-applied force, adjusting motor behaviour based on input, thereby validating the functionality of the active control system.

From the initial electrical architecture, an LDO was required to step down the 5 V from the buck converter to 3.3 V to supply power to the IMUs and load cells (Figure 54).

However, from the ET1 test, the mean voltage output of the LDO and 3.3 V output pin of the Raspberry Pi Pico 2W were of the same value at 3.29 V. 4.3.5.2 Force Sensing Electrical Schematic Revision Iteration

Another modification was the use of load cells for our force sensing instead of the force sensitive resistors that were initially proposed (Figure 55).

Figure 55.

As shown above, FSRs complicated the electrical architecture due to its non-linear behaviour and its small area, requiring an op-amp to linearise the voltage output for easier conversion using the analog-to-digital pins of the microcontroller. Lastly, an analog multiplexer was also required to sample a large number of FSRs due to its small surface area, increasing wiring complexity. As such, replacing them with load cells simplified the electrical architecture as it only needed an HX711 amplifier which linearises the analog output, and has a larger surface area compared to FSR, reducing the overall components used (Figure 56).

4.4.1 BT1 Combined Bluetooth Latency and Data Sampling Rate Test

The data analysis system consists of a Bluetooth-enabled pipeline between the Pico microcontroller and the Kinetix UI, where sensor data (IMU angles and force readings) are sampled on the device and transmitted in real time. The Pico aggregates and formats this data into structured packets (e.g., TICK and DATA messages), which are sent at a controlled rate of 5 Hz. On the UI side, incoming data is parsed, visualised, and stored, enabling real-time monitoring, latency evaluation, and post-session analysis such as graph generation and CSV export. A diagnostic module was used to evaluate communication performance by measuring end-to-end latency and packet reliability through controlled test sequences (Figure 59).

Figure 59.

Although the operational update rate is 1 Hz, the system was validated at 5 Hz to ensure robustness under higher data transmission demands. Result:

In the initial implementation, Kinetix adopted a single-core architecture where Bluetooth communication, motor control, and sensor processing were executed within the same processing loop. This resulted in blocking behaviour, particularly during motor actuation where delays (e.g., sleep_us) prevented the BLE stack from being serviced in a timely manner. Consequently, the system experienced high latency (up to 564 ms) and frequent Bluetooth disconnections, as the communication subsystem failed to meet required servicing intervals (Figure 60).

Figure 60.

Design iteration:

To address this limitation, the system architecture was redesigned into a dual-core distributed architecture (Figure 61). In the improved design:

  1. Core 0 is dedicated to handling BLE communication and command processing, ensuring continuous and non-blocking servicing of the Bluetooth stack.
  2. Core 1 is responsible for motor control, sensor acquisition (IMU and force sensors), and data processing, where blocking operations can be safely executed without affecting communication performance.
  3. A shared memory interface was introduced to enable communication between the two cores.

Figure 61.

Commands received via BLE are written to shared variables (e.g., motor requests), while processed sensor data is updated by Core 1 and read by Core 0 for transmission to the UI. Following these changes, the test was repeated using a diagnostics module that tested the throughput of 25 packets over 5 trials at 5 Hz (Figure 62 and Table 29).

4.5.1 Design Iteration

Testing for the User Interface Sub-System was conducted through user tests in Section 6.4.

Iteration 2 focused on simplifying the interface, improving therapist workflow, and enhancing client engagement through gamification and real-time feedback.
