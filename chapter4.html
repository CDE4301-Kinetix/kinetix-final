To address this issue, the design was modified by relocating the arc rail beneath the linear actuator and the main structural sub-assembly. 4.1.8 Mechanical Design Short comings

4.1.8.1 Middle Structure Section

While assembling the overall device for the first time there were issues with the stability middle section with the supports starting to bend due to the vertical load. On top of that, the mounting holes for the section are hard to reach thus making it hard for any maintenance (Figure 29).

Figure 28.

To resolve this issue, ribs were added to the structure and mountings were shifted towards a more accessible angle.

4.1.8.2 Belt Lateral mechanism

During the development phase, tightening issues were encountered with the belt used for the lateral mechanism (Figure 30).

Figure 29.

The mechanism consists of two pulleys connected by a timing belt, with one pulley mounted to the stepper motor and the other to the main structure. To address tensioning issues, a belt tensioner was initially designed and implemented using 3D-printed components (Figure 31).

Figure 30.

The 3D printed parts did not manage to sustain the friction and bending force. Thus, we opted to use a shorter timing belt which resolved this issue.

4.1.8.3 Lateral Driving Shaft

Another issue encountered involved the lateral shaft connected to the main structure for side-to-side motion. Initially, a shaft torque lock was used to secure the shaft through outward expansion; however, this was later replaced with a nut-based fastening method to provide a more stable and reliable fixation (Figure 32).

Figure 31.

The limitation of this design was that it relied solely on friction between the 3D-printed components and the shaft, resulting in insufficient clamping force and slippage. To resolve this, the system was revised to use a flange coupler with mounting holes, and the shaft was changed to a 6 mm round shaft with a field D-profile end to improve torque transmission and mechanical locking (Figure 33).

Figure 32.

The D-profile was introduced to provide a flat surface for the flange coupler’s set screw, ensuring a more secure grip and preventing slippage. As a result, the mechanism was able to achieve lateral movement.

While the ideal MSE is below 12° to outperform “by-eye” clinical judgment, our results showed that the IMU was not able to consistently achieve this error (Figure 36).

The IMU signal exhibits both overestimation and underestimation of joint angles at critical moments. These inconsistencies contribute to elevated error values, preventing reliable adherence to the <12° MSE benchmark. Full results are contained in Appendix K-N due to the volume of data.

4.2.4 Test Shortcoming and Proposals

The observed angular errors are suspected to arise from reliance on accelerometer data alone, which is sensitive to dynamic acceleration components during movement. When the limb is in motion, non-gravitational accelerations are superimposed on the gravitational signal, causing the computed joint angles to deviate from the true orientation.

To address this, future iterations should incorporate gyroscope data alongside the accelerometer through a sensor fusion approach such as a Kalman filter, which combines the low-frequency accuracy of the accelerometer with the short-term precision of the gyroscope to produce more stable and reliable angle estimates across the full range of motion.

Additionally, the current test configuration did not constrain the physical mounting of the IMU modules, introducing variability in sensor tilt and alignment between trials. A dedicated IMU holder, designed to mount rigidly onto the device frame and align each sensor consistently with the limb segment axis, would reduce placement-dependent error and improve repeatability across patients and sessions. The design of such a holder is proposed as part of the sensing subsystem iteration.

4.5 Sensing Design Concepts

Three sensing subsystem concepts were evaluated.

4.5.1 FIMU (Force + IMU) Design Concept

The IMU subsystem combines an accelerometer, gyroscope, and optionally a magnetometer to measure motion and angular velocity across three axes. This concept employs a four-IMU setup (lower back, thigh, shank, and foot).

Force sensors target key pressure points on the foot: hallux, metatarsals, midfoot, and heels. For medial-lateral force sensing, sensors are placed near the medial and lateral malleoli.

Figure 31. FIMU sensor placement for positional and force sensing.

Figure 32

4.5.2 Hall-Force-Flex (HFF) Design Concept

The HFF subsystem combines Hall-effect sensors, force sensors, and flex sensors to capture biomechanical data. These sensors are positioned at key regions for hip/knee positional sensing, plantar force sensing, lateral force sensing, and ankle positional sensing.

Figure 33
HFF sensor placement — Force sensors (orange), Hall sensors (blue), and Flex sensors (pink) at hip/knee, lateral, plantar, and ankle positions.

Figure 33. HFF sensor placement diagram.

4.5.3 Computer Vision Design Concept

A computer vision sensor uses an RGB camera and pose-estimation algorithms to track 3D joint positions (e.g., hip, knee, ankle) and compute joint angles from relative body segment orientations. Markers are placed on key joints, and movements are processed using Tracker Software to determine marker positions and calculate joint angles.

4.7 Electrical Design

For motor selection, the maximum torque that the screw bar can resist before backdriving must be determined. Using a 150 kg load and a 10 mm screw lead:

Tb = (F · P · η2) / 2π

Where F = load, P = screw lead, η₂ = efficiency of the bar (assumed 0.9). Calculated backdriving torque = 2.15 Nm.

4.7.4 Simplified Electrical Schematic

The power architecture flows as follows:

  1. 230V / 50Hz wall plug → AC-DC Power Adapter → 48 VDC for motors.
  2. DC-DC Buck Converter → 5 VDC for the MCU (Raspberry Pi Pico W2).
  3. Low-Dropout Regulator (LDO) → 3.3 VDC for sensors (IMU, force sensors).

Communication protocols: IMU uses I2C via multiplexer; force sensors and other peripherals use standard GPIO and ADC.

The MCU connects to the tablet/smartphone via Wi-Fi for user interface communication.
